Abstract

Reinforcement learning for reasoning LLMs stalls when problem difficulty drifts off the model's capability frontier: too-easy rollout groups are all-correct and too-hard groups all-wrong, both yielding zero group-relative gradient. RLVE keeps problems solvable through procedurally generated verifiable environments with adaptive difficulty, but uses a fixed rule — raise difficulty once accuracy exceeds 0.9 — which parks the model in a low-signal regime. We recast difficulty selection as action selection under the Free Energy Principle: maintain a posterior over the model's latent competence per environment and choose the difficulty that minimizes the Expected Free Energy (EFE), trading off the pragmatic learning signal against the epistemic information gain about competence. The resulting policy π(d) ∝ exp(−G(d)/T) targets the variance-maximizing success band (p ≈ 0.5) while exploring when uncertain. Reproducing RLVE from ProRL-1.5B-v2 on a single GPU, we compare static, RLVE-threshold, a signal-only ablation, and the full FEP controller on training-time effective sample ratio and held-out generalization.

1Introduction

RL post-training improves LLM reasoning, but progress slows when the training signal vanishes. For group-relative methods (GRPO/DAPO), a prompt is informative only if its group of K rollouts is non-degenerate; an all-correct or all-wrong group has zero within-group reward variance and contributes no gradient. The probability a binary-reward group is informative is UK(p)=1−pK−(1−p)K, maximized at success rate p=0.5. Keeping the model near this band is therefore the central objective of adaptive-difficulty training.

RLVE [1] supplies this via RLVE-Gym, 400 hand-engineered verifiable environments that generate unbounded instances with algorithmic verifiers and a difficulty knob d. Its controller, however, is a one-directional heuristic: per environment it raises the difficulty upper bound once accuracy at that bound exceeds τacc=0.9. This operates the model near p≈0.9, where UK(0.9)≈0.57 ≪ UK(0.5)≈0.99 (K=8) — most groups are wasted — and it never lowers difficulty, has no explicit objective, and no exploration term.

Contributions. (i) We formalize difficulty selection as Expected-Free-Energy minimization and show RLVE's 0.9 rule is a low-signal operating point (§3, Fig. 1). (ii) We give an online, belief-based controller — a drop-in for RLVE's difficulty scheduler — with closed-form pragmatic and epistemic terms (§3). (iii) We design a four-arm comparison, isolating the epistemic (FEP) contribution via a signal-only ablation (§4).

2Background

2.1 Verifiable environments and GRPO/DAPO

An RLVE environment is a program with a procedural generator, an algorithmic verifier, and an integer difficulty d≥0. Training draws a batch of prompts, samples K rollouts each, and updates the policy with group-relative advantages; DAPO discards zero-variance groups via dynamic sampling, so the effective rollouts are exactly those in informative groups.

2.2 RLVE's adaptive-difficulty rule

Per environment, RLVE keeps a range [ℓ,h], samples d∼Uniform(ℓ,h), tracks accuracy a/b at the upper bound h, and sets h←h+1 once b≥τnum and a/b≥0.9 (sliding window dΔ=4). The update is one-directional and rule-based.

3Method: FEP-RLVE

We treat "which difficulty to train on" as an action chosen to minimize Expected Free Energy. The correspondence to active inference [2,3]:

hidden state smodel's latent competence in an environment
belief q(s)posterior over competence (with uncertainty)
observation oa rollout at difficulty d is correct / wrong
action πwhich difficulty d to pose
EFE G(d)expected cost of posing d (signal + info + budget)

3.1 Expected Free Energy and the policy

For each candidate difficulty we score its EFE and sample by the standard active-inference policy prior:

Ge(d) = − λsig·UK(p̂) − λinfo·I(s;o|d) + λcost·C(d)(1)

π(d|e) ∝ exp( − Ge(d) / T )(2)

Pragmatic value is the effective prompt ratio UK(p)=1−pK−(1−p)K (the DAPO signal), maximal at p=0.5. Epistemic value is the expected information gain about competence,

I(s;o|d) = H[q(s)] − Eo∼p(o|d) H[q(s|o,d)](3)

which is largest at boundary difficulties that best discriminate competence — also near p≈0.5. Thus both terms point at the signal-optimal band by independent arguments (Fig. 1). The temperature T sets exploitation vs exploration: T→0 recovers a hard set-point at the optimal 0.5 (vs RLVE's 0.9), T→∞ a uniform difficulty distribution.

3.2 Competence belief and online control

We model competence with an IRT-style likelihood P(correct|s,d)=σ(a(s−d)) on a grid, updated by Bayes after each rollout. Per step: (1) compute G(d) for each level; (2) sample d∼π, generate; (3) verify; (4) update q(s). When the belief is uncertain the epistemic term dominates and difficulty exploration widens; once competence is localized the pragmatic term dominates — explore→exploit emerges without hand-tuned annealing. Setting λinfo=0 yields a signal-only ablation (Signal-RLVE).

6Discussion and Limitations

When the model outgrows the maximum difficulty, every level saturates and the signal collapses for any controller — motivating environment scaling, RLVE's central thesis, as the natural extension. For partial-credit (unit-test-fraction) rewards the variance-maximizing point shifts off 0.5, where the explicit utility UK is a cleaner target than a hand-set threshold. The competence model is a single scalar per environment; a richer multi-skill belief is future work.

7Conclusion

Casting difficulty selection as Expected-Free-Energy minimization replaces RLVE's accuracy-threshold heuristic with a principled, single-knob controller that targets the signal-optimal operating point and actively infers model competence, sustaining more learning signal at equal compute.
